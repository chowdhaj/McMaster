Calendar Description

Unix and shell programming, makefiles, version control; assembly basics, translating high-level language into assembly, parameter passing, arrays, recursion; compiling, debugging, profiling, and software optimizations.
Two lectures, one lab (three hours per week), first term
Prerequisite(s):
COMPSCI 1MD3 or ENGINEER 1D04 or IBEHS 1P10
Co-requisite(s):
COMPSCI 2S03
Antirequisite(s):
SFWRENG 2XA3

Course Objectives

The objective is to allow students to acquire the fundamental software development skills.

Outline of Topics

  • Unix commands
  • The BASH shell
  • Makefiles
  • Hardware basics
  • Assembly basics
  • Subprograms
  • Recursive subprograms
  • Bit operations
  • Arrays

About this course

This is a new type of course designed to enhance experiential learning at McMaster University. The course has two hours of lectures and 3 hours of labs each week. Majority of the work is thus done by the students in the labs under a supervision of the TA's during the Minor Labs. The Major Labs are devoted to in-lab projects when the students are given a problem to work on and submit electronically their work at the end of the session. To view the problem and to be able to submit your solution, you have to be physically in the lab room assigned to your lab section (different section will work on different problems). For serious reasons (such as accommodation or health issues or participation at hackathon out of town) the students may be allowed to participate in Major Labs remotely -- this must be arranged with the instructor or the head TA. The electronic submission can be done either via the course website (note that the course website is not on Avenue !!! ) or via line command on the server. There will be an individual final project that will be also submitted electronically. On the final project you can work at home and for its submission you do not need to be in the labs.
  • Cooperation among students in the course is strongly encouraged during minor labs, however the major lab projects are to be individual effort -- the solution to the problem must be your own to count; solutions designed with help of others (including the ones copied from the internet) will be treated as a case of academic dishonesty, see below.
  • The final project is also an individual effort and no co-operation will be tolerated.

Academic Dishonesty

Academic dishonesty consists of misrepresentation by deception or by other fraudulent means and can result in serious consequences, e.g. the grade of zero on an assignment, loss of credit with a notation on the transcript (notation reads: "Grade of F assigned for academic dishonesty"), and/or suspension or expulsion from the university. It is the student's responsibility to understand what constitutes academic dishonesty.

For information on the various kinds of a academic dishonesty please refer to the Academic Integrity Policy, specifically Appendix 3, located at http://www.mcmaster.ca/policy/Students-AcademicStudies/AcademicIntegrity.pdf
